## MISSION

Bahamas Premier Girls Fastpitch is an elite youth softball ecosystem committed to revolutionizing softball in The Bahamas while developing the next generation of Bahamian girls on and off the field.

## VISION

Bahamas Premier exists to become the premier softball organization in The Bahamas and a leading model for youth softball development across the Caribbean.

The vision is to create a long-term ecosystem where Bahamian girls can access quality coaching, age-appropriate development, competitive opportunity, mentorship, leadership development, and pathways to higher-level softball without having to leave the country to find it.

## FOUR KEY PILLARS

Every program, every practice, and every moment is built on these foundational principles.

Grow the Game
Increase national access and participation in softball by introducing more girls to the sport earlier and lowering barriers to entry.

Raise the Standard
Elevate how softball is trained, coached, officiated, and played across The Bahamas.

Develop the Whole Girl
Use softball as a vehicle to build confident, disciplined, educated, God-fearing, and empowered young women.

Bring Opportunity Home
Create in-country pathways for elite training, competition, and exposure so Bahamian softball players can succeed within their own borders.

## DEVELOPMENTAL GAP IN THE BAHAMAS

Currently, there are limited structured softball programs for girls under age 10.

Most Bahamian girls begin softball around ages 11 to 13.

Girls in countries like the United States, Japan, and Canada often begin around ages 4 to 6.

This delayed start creates a gap in skill development, confidence, game IQ, athletic development, and long-term opportunity.

Bahamas Premier is closing that gap.

## WHY WE EXIST

Many young Bahamian girls have no clear entry point into softball, with most youth programs focused on baseball.

Limited access to structured girls' sports programs has contributed to low participation in softball and sports in general for young Bahamian girls.

Most Bahamian girls start softball later than their international peers, limiting long-term development opportunities.

The overall quality of the game must improve, from player development to coaching, officiating, and culture, to create a better experience and more opportunities for future generations.

Too often, players must leave the country to access elite training, competition, and recruiting opportunities.

Bahamas Premier is creating a system designed to close these gaps and create a stronger future for softball in The Bahamas.

## FOUNDER BIO

Use this to create the founder page.

Shawnté Curtis is a former national team athlete and present coach dedicated to transforming the future of softball in The Bahamas.

Her journey began at just five years old at the Freedom Farm Baseball League, where she played baseball in the absence of opportunities for girls in softball. For years, she competed alongside boys, often as the only girl on her team and in entire tournaments, gaining firsthand experience in the realities of limited access for young female athletes. That experience would later shape her vision to create a space specifically for young Bahamian girls in softball.

After transitioning to softball at age 12, Shawnté went on to represent The Bahamas on both the Junior and Senior National Teams, while also competing in travel ball. Through international competition, she saw a consistent gap in development, structure, and resources between Bahamian athletes and their global counterparts, fueling her belief that the sport must be intentionally developed at home.

Although she did not have the guidance needed to pursue collegiate softball, Shawnté earned a degree in Sport Administration and remained committed to the game. She began her professional coaching career in Atlanta, working as an instructor at a baseball and softball training facility and coaching travel teams. There, she gained exposure to high-level training environments and saw firsthand what it takes to develop elite athletes.

Softball has been a defining force in Shawnté's life, providing opportunities, discipline, and life lessons that extend far beyond the field. Today, through Bahamas Premier, she is focused on elevating the entire softball ecosystem and raising the standard of the sport in The Bahamas.

## EQUIPMENT GUIDE

Headline:
Equipment Guide

Intro:
You do not need top-of-the-line equipment to begin. Our focus is helping athletes develop a love for the game, learn proper fundamentals, and build good habits. We recommend choosing equipment that fits your athlete properly rather than buying oversized items to “grow into.”

Required Equipment:

Softball Glove
Recommended glove sizes:
6U, ages 5 to 6: 10 inches to 10.5 inches
8U, ages 7 to 8: 10.5 inches to 11 inches
10U, ages 9 to 10: 11 inches to 11.5 inches

Glove Tips:
For a right-handed athlete, purchase a glove that fits the left hand.
For a left-handed athlete, purchase a glove that fits the right hand.
Choose a glove your athlete can comfortably open and close.
Softer, game-ready gloves are best for beginners.
Avoid oversized gloves.
Keep your receipt in case you need to exchange.

Athletic Clothing:
Recommended: athletic shorts, tights, or softball pants, preferably black, and athletic socks or softball socks.
Avoid: jeans, dresses, skirts, and open-toed shoes.

Athletic Shoes or Cleats:
Recommended: athletic tennis shoes or youth softball/baseball molded cleats.
Cleats are especially recommended for 10U athletes because they will participate in more game-like activities and begin preparing for 12U+ play.
Avoid: metal cleats.

Softball Face Mask:
Face masks provide added protection for fielders from batted balls and high-speed throws. They can also help timid fielders feel more confident.

Recommended Equipment:
Softball pants and socks
Batting helmet with face mask
Personal bat
Softball equipment backpack

Bat Size Guide:
6U, ages 5 to 6: 24 inches to 26 inches
8U, ages 7 to 8: 26 inches to 28 inches
10U, ages 9 to 10: 28 inches to 30 inches

Simple Bat Test:
Have your athlete hold the bat straight out with one arm for 10 seconds. If she can hold it comfortably, it is likely a good size. If she struggles heavily, it is likely too heavy.

Equipment Note:
Expensive equipment does not automatically make athletes better. Proper fit and comfort matter much more than price. Label everything with your athlete's name.

Explain the developmental gap in The Bahamas and why Bahamas Premier was created.

Final Copy:

Why We Exist

Many young Bahamian girls have no clear entry point into softball. Most youth programs focus on baseball, and many girls do not begin softball until later than their international peers.

In countries like the United States, Japan, and Canada, girls are often introduced to softball around ages 4 to 6. In The Bahamas, many girls begin around ages 11 to 13. That delayed start creates a gap in confidence, skill development, game IQ, athletic development, and long-term opportunity.

Bahamas Premier exists to close that gap.

We are creating a structured pathway that introduces girls to the game earlier, supports their development over time, and helps raise the overall standard of softball in The Bahamas.

This is about more than participation. It is about access, development, opportunity, and building a stronger future for the sport.

## PAGE 7: MEET THE FOUNDER

Purpose:
Build trust and credibility through Shawnté Curtis' story.

Final Copy:

Founded by Shawnté Curtis
Former National Team Athlete. Coach. Builder of the Future of Bahamian Softball.

Shawnté Curtis is a former national team athlete and present coach dedicated to transforming the future of softball in The Bahamas.

Her journey began at just five years old at the Freedom Farm Baseball League, where she played baseball in the absence of opportunities for girls in softball. For years, she competed alongside boys, often as the only girl on her team and in entire tournaments. That experience gave her firsthand understanding of the limited access young female athletes often face.

After transitioning to softball at age 12, Shawnté went on to represent The Bahamas on both the Junior and Senior National Teams while also competing in travel ball. Through international competition, she saw a consistent gap in development, structure, and resources between Bahamian athletes and their global counterparts.

Although she did not have the guidance needed to pursue collegiate softball, Shawnté earned a degree in Sport Administration and remained committed to the game. She later began her professional coaching career in Atlanta, working as an instructor at a baseball and softball training facility and coaching travel teams.

Today, through Bahamas Premier, Shawnté is focused on elevating the entire softball ecosystem and raising the standard of the sport in The Bahamas.
